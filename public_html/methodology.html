How We Rank News

Most news feeds fail. They drown you in noise. They duplicate the same story a dozen times. They let one outlet dominate the conversation.

Project RollUp is different. We surface signals, not headlines. Here's how.

01 — The Signal Problem

News aggregators typically just merge RSS feeds and call it a day. The result:

  • You see the same story 15 times from different outlets
  • Breaking news drowns out important slow-burn stories
  • One publisher's editorial bias can dominate your feed
  • No way to know if a story is confirmed or just one outlet's take

We built RollUp to fix that.

02 — How We Rank

Every story gets a composite score based on four factors:

  • Recency (35%) — Breaking news matters, but not at the expense of everything else
  • Source diversity (25%) — Stories confirmed by multiple outlets rank higher
  • Credibility (25%) — Not all sources are equal; we weight by track record
  • Cross-confirmation (15%) — Stories appearing in multiple feeds with matching facts get a boost

No single factor dominates. A breaking story from one outlet won't bury a well-confirmed story from six.

03 — Clustering

When you see 4 SOURCES on a card, that means we found four outlets reporting the same event with enough similarity to cluster them.

We use fuzzy matching on headlines and summaries to identify duplicates. Similar stories get grouped. One representative story surfaces. The rest are suppressed.

Result: you see the story once, with a source count that tells you how widely it's confirmed.

04 — Bias Reduction

No outlet gets to dominate the feed. If a single publisher has 10 stories in the top 50, we apply a balance penalty to prevent monoculture.

We also filter out opinion pieces, op-eds, and editorial content unless they're reporting new facts. Commentary doesn't rank.

Sources are weighted by credibility scores based on fact-checking records, corrections policies, and editorial standards.

05 — What We Don't Claim

We are not fully unbiased. No news system is.

Our source list reflects editorial choices. Our ranking weights reflect values (we prioritize confirmation over speed, for example).

What we do claim: we're transparent about our methodology, we don't let one outlet dominate, and we show you how many sources confirm each story.

06 — Source Standards

To qualify for the feed, an outlet must:

  • Have a public corrections policy
  • Publish original reporting (not just aggregation)
  • Maintain editorial independence from political parties
  • Have a track record longer than 2 years

We don't include random blogs, partisan outlets with no fact-checking, or sources that fail basic editorial standards.

Our source list is public. If you think we're missing a credible outlet, let us know.

07 — In Practice

Here's what you get:

  • One story per event, not ten duplicates
  • Source counts so you know how confirmed it is
  • Confidence scores based on cross-source agreement
  • No opinion pieces unless they're reporting new facts
  • No single outlet dominating the conversation

The feed updates every few minutes. Stories re-rank as new sources confirm them. Breaking news surfaces fast, but doesn't bury everything else.

08 — Bottom Line

Project RollUp is a disciplined intelligence terminal. Not a chatbot. Not a content farm. Not a clone of every other news site.

We surface signals. We cluster noise. We show you what's confirmed.

That's it.
